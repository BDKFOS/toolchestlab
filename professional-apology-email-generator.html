When to use it

Use this when you need to apologize for a workplace mistake, delayed response, missed deadline, scheduling issue, project delay, client problem, or misunderstanding without making the message sound bigger than it needs to be.

What makes a good professional apology

A good apology is specific, calm, and accountable. It names what happened, avoids excuses, acknowledges the impact, and gives the recipient a clear next step when one is needed.

Keep it accountable but not dramatic

The best workplace apologies do not over-explain, over-apologize, or sound fake. Take responsibility, keep the tone steady, and focus on what you are doing to fix or prevent the issue.
